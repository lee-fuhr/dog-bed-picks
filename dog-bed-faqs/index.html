Dog Bed questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Choosing the right dog bed comes down to your dog’s size, sleeping style, and health needs. Look for durable materials, easy-to-clean covers, and ample support. The most important factor is matching the bed to how your dog actually sleeps.

What size dog bed should I buy?

Start by measuring your dog from nose to tail while they’re standing naturally. Then add six to twelve inches to account for stretching and sprawl. A dog that curls into a tight ball can get away with a smaller bed, while a side-sleeper or one that stretches out will need more room. Most brands provide size charts linked to weight ranges, but those can be misleading for long-backed breeds like Dachshunds or deep-chested ones like Greyhounds. Owner forums often suggest going up a size rather than down. A bed that’s too small can discourage use, while a slightly oversized bed feels luxurious.

Orthopedic vs memory foam: which is better for my dog?

Orthopedic foam, usually high-density polyfoam, offers consistent, firm support that doesn’t change shape under your dog’s weight. It’s ideal for dogs with arthritis, hip dysplasia, or general joint issues because it keeps the spine aligned without sagging. Memory foam contours to the body, relieving pressure points, but it can trap heat and may soften in warmer rooms. Many premium beds combine a thick memory-foam layer with a supportive orthopedic base. For senior dogs, a bed with at least four inches of dense foam tends to hold up best, based on long-term owner reviews. Look for foam that is CertiPUR-US certified for safety and durability. If your dog runs hot, a gel-infused memory foam or a ventilated design can help with temperature regulation.

How do I clean my dog’s bed?

The easiest beds to clean have removable, machine-washable covers. Most covers can be washed in warm water on a gentle cycle and tumble dried low. Foam cores should never go in the washer; spot clean them with mild detergent and water, then air dry completely to prevent mold and mildew. For odor control, sprinkle baking soda over the cover or foam, let it sit for fifteen minutes, then vacuum. A waterproof liner between the cover and foam can protect the core from accidents and slobber. Some owners keep two covers to rotate between washes.

How often should I replace my dog’s bed?

A good-quality dog bed typically lasts one to two years with daily use. Signs that it’s time to replace include visible sagging or lumps, foam that no longer springs back after pressing, a torn cover that can’t be repaired, or your dog starting to sleep elsewhere. Chewers tend to go through beds faster, sometimes every few months. Rotating two beds can extend each one’s life, especially for dogs that spend a lot of time in a crate or on a dog couch. High-density foam and heavy-duty fabrics from brands like Big Barker or Kuranda can last several years if cared for properly. If your dog is aging and the bed feels thin, upgrading to a thicker orthopedic model can make a real difference in their comfort.

What materials are best for dogs that chew?

For determined chewers, look for beds made from ballistic nylon, Cordura, or heavy-duty canvas. These fabrics resist teeth better than standard polyester fleece. A chew-resistant zipper, often with a flap covering the zipper track, is another important detail because many dogs target the seam. Avoid beds with loose stuffing or thin seams that can be easily ripped open. K9 Ballistics and the Kuranda aluminum-frame bed with a removable vinyl cover are two options frequently recommended on owner forums for destructive chewers. No bed is indestructible, though. If your dog is a persistent chewer, supervise their access to the bed, and consider a raised cot-style bed that’s harder to grip and gives less purchase for shredding.

Does my dog need a bed in their crate?

Yes, a crate bed adds cushioning and warmth, making the crate a more inviting space. The bed should fit snugly, not so large that it bunches against the walls or so small that your dog can’t stretch comfortably. Thin, flat mats or bolsters that can’t be folded up are safest for dogs that might start chewing. Look for crate beds with a non-slip bottom so they stay in place. Many crates are used in cars or during travel, so a washable, quick-drying option is practical. For puppies, a bed with a waterproof layer protects against accidents. Some owners skip the bed entirely if their dog tends to chew everything, but even a simple fleece blanket can serve as a temporary solution.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a human bed or pillow for my dog?

Yes, but it’s not ideal because human pillows lack the orthopedic support many dogs need, and they wear out faster. A dedicated dog bed provides better joint alignment and is usually easier to clean.

Are raised dog beds better than floor beds?

Raised cots are excellent for airflow and cooling, making them a good choice for hot climates or dogs with thick coats. They also discourage chewing and provide firm support, but some older dogs prefer the cushion of an orthopedic floor bed.

How do I get my dog to use their new bed?

Place the bed where your dog already likes to nap, like near a window or in your bedroom. Add a favorite blanket or toy, and reward them with treats or praise when they lie on it. Patience works best, most dogs accept a new bed within a few days.

Is a heated dog bed safe?

Heated beds are generally safe if they’re low-voltage and have an automatic shutoff. They can soothe sore joints and provide comfort in cold weather. Avoid using them with puppies or dogs that chew cords, and always follow the manufacturer’s safety instructions.

What’s the best dog bed for a senior dog?

Senior dogs benefit from a thick, high-density foam bed, either orthopedic or memory foam, with a low entry edge so they can step on and off easily. Look for a bed with a supportive base and a washable cover. Many owners also recommend a bed with bolsters for neck and back support.

Do dog beds help with anxiety?

Yes, a bed with high sides or a “donut” shape can create a feeling of security for anxious dogs. The enclosed shape mimics a den-like environment. Adding a familiar scent, like a worn t-shirt, can also help calm them.

In shortThe right dog bed is a long-term investment in your pet’s comfort and health, it’s one product where cheap often costs more in replacements and vet bills later. Focus on size, material quality, and your dog’s specific sleeping habits, and you’ll find a bed that earns a deep sigh of approval.
