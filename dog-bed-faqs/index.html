Dog Bed questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best dog bed depends on your dog’s size, sleeping style, health needs, and your home. Prioritize orthopedic support for older or achy dogs, easy-to-clean covers for messy pups, and a tough exterior for chewers. Measure your dog while sprawled and add a few inches to find the right fit.

What size dog bed should I get?

Measure your dog from nose to tail while they’re stretched out in their favourite sleeping position, then add 6–8 inches for wiggle room. A bed that’s too small means your dog will hang off the edges; one that’s too large may feel drafty or overwhelming. Consider where the bed will go. If it’s for a crate, the bed must fit inside without curling up at the corners. For open spaces, go with the larger size your dog will comfortably use. Most retailers list dimensions and recommended weight ranges, but always use your measurements as the final guide.

Orthopedic vs. standard: which is better?

Orthopedic beds use high-density memory foam or medical-grade foam to distribute weight evenly and relieve pressure on joints. They’re ideal for senior dogs, breeds prone to hip dysplasia, or any dog that sleeps flat for long periods. The foam holds its shape longer than polyfill, but these beds are heavier and cost a bit more. Standard polyfill beds are fluffy, lightweight, and generally more affordable. They suit young, healthy dogs who like to burrow or nest. The trade-off is that polyfill flattens over time and offers less support. For a growing puppy that will be replaced in a couple of years, a standard bed is fine. For a dog you want to keep comfortable into old age, orthopedic is worth the investment.

How do I choose between a bolster bed and a flat mat?

Watch how your dog sleeps at home. If they curl into a tight ball or rest their head on furniture, a bolster bed (with raised edges) gives them a pillow-like surface to lean against. This can also make them feel more secure. Flat mats or cots are better for dogs that sprawl out on their belly or side, especially those that tend to overheat. There’s no wrong choice, but note that bolsters can be more difficult to clean and may not fit in some crates. Some dogs prefer the openness of a flat bed. You can also try a hybrid that has low bolsters on two or three sides.

What material is best for a dog bed cover?

Look for a cover that is removable and machine-washable. Microfiber or brushed polyester holds up well to washing and feels soft, while cotton canvas is breathable and durable. If your dog drools, sheds heavily, or has accidents, a waterproof inner liner (often a separate layer under the cover) is non-negotiable. Avoid fabrics that trap hair, like fleece or velour, unless you’re ready to lint-roll constantly. Also check that the zipper is sturdy and can be replaced. A cover that unzips fully for easy removal is better than one with a small opening.

How often should I wash the bed?

Aim to wash the removable cover every two weeks, or weekly if your dog is messy or has allergies. Spot-clean the foam or filler between washes with a mild enzyme cleaner to remove odours. Some beds come with a waterproof liner that wipes down easily. Neglecting washing can lead to lingering smells, skin irritation, and flea infestations. If you notice the foam starting to yellow or crumble, it’s time to replace the entire bed. Most manufacturers recommend replacing the bed every 2–5 years depending on use and care.

What about waterproof or chew-proof beds?

Waterproof beds have a sealed liner that prevents liquid from soaking into the foam. They’re essential for incontinent senior dogs, puppies in training, or dogs that hork water after a walk. Look for a bed that lists “waterproof” in the construction details, not just the removable cover. Chew-proof beds use tough fabrics like ballistic nylon or ripstop polyester with a double-stitched seam. No bed is fully indestructible, but these are much harder to tear. If your dog is a determined chewer, choose a bed with a removable cover so you can replace just the cover if needed. Also consider a bed that comes with a chew-through warranty.

Frequently asked questions

Can my dog sleep on a human bed instead of a dog bed?

It’s fine as long as your dog can get on and off safely, but a human mattress doesn’t provide the same joint support as a high-density foam dog bed. Many owners let their dog co-sleep and still offer a dedicated dog bed as a daytime lounging spot.

How long does a good dog bed last?

Expect 2–5 years from a quality bed. Memory foam beds hold up longer than polyfill, and removable covers extend life because you can wash them. Signs of wear include flattened foam, torn seams, or permanent odours.

Should I get a heated dog bed?

Only if your vet recommends it for arthritis or poor circulation. Otherwise, a well-insulated bed is safer. Self-warming beds (using reflective material) are a good alternative that don’t rely on electricity.

Is memory foam safe for dogs?

Yes, provided it’s CertiPUR-US certified or similar, meaning no harmful chemicals or heavy metals. Avoid cheap foam that may off-gas. High-density memory foam retains shape better and is less likely to sag.

Do dog beds come with a warranty?

Many brands offer a 1–3 year warranty covering manufacturing defects and foam compression. Some premium beds have longer warranties, but read the fine print: normal wear and tear or damage from chewing is usually excluded.

What is the best shape for a dog that curls up?

A donut or bolster bed with raised edges gives a cozy nest feel. Dogs that curl up often like to lean on something while they sleep. Oval or round bolsters work best, but flat rectangle beds with bolsters on two sides can also suffice.

In shortTake a few days to watch your dog’s sleeping habits before buying. Look for signs of joint pain, preference for soft or firm surfaces, and any tendencies to chew or dig. A well-chosen bed pays for itself in better sleep, fewer aches, and an easier-to-clean home.
